FEB 2024 • MASTER THESIS PROJECT

Guidelines for complex AAC systems with LLMs

This project proposes Human-Computer Interaction guidelines for researchers, designers and developers that want to explore the use of AI language models to assist individuals with complex communication needs. Secondary studies were conducted to examines the existing contributions and lists opportunities and challenges. The primary research followed a co-design approach and engaged AAC users and familiar listeners who shared their expectations, concerns, and ideas about future LLM integration into AAC devices. This analytical and critical work was developed between May 2023 and February 2024, in collaboration with the Reality and Multimedia Research Group (UFPE, Recife, Brazil) and the Inclusive Design Research Centre (OCAD, Toronto, Canada).

NOV 2022 • DUAL-SECTOR RESEARCH (INDUSTRY AND GOVERNMENTAL PROJECT)

National Rural Environmental Registry

This is a Foundational research I lead at YouX Group between July and November 2022. As part of a governmental policy, this project aims at geo-referencing all Brazilian rural properties to promote controlling, monitoring, and combating deforestation of native vegetation. The system includes more than 7 million registries that cover 672 million hectares of national territory. Our mixed-methods approach allowed us to identify and map the particular pain points of each of the 27 Brazilian federative units (states) and propose applicable solutions to their main issues. The work summarized complex research findings into straightforward deliverables, which were used during multiple governmental meetings and allowed for quick decision-making. Our results not only impacted the brazilian green agenda, but also the client, as our collaborative approach strengthened the relationship between the federal government and all state's staff. The final findings were proudly presented during the Brazil at the National Conference of the Rural Environmental Registry.

MAY 2024 • CONFERENCE PAPER

Artificially Intelligent and Implicitly Ableist: Analyzing Language Model Bias Towards Persons with Disabilities

Abstract: Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools have gained much attention recently, and their applications are widespread in different domains, including private and public sectors. While these technologies can be harnessed for beneficial uses, they can also display undesired discrimination. The risks of AI bias are particularly concerning as it holds the power to perpetuate or amplify existing harms against minoritized groups. This work aims to contribute to the ongoing discussion of biased AI, specifically in language models. The study analyzes bias, the risks it poses to persons with disabilities and provides suggestions for building more inclusive and equitable AI technologies. Next, a simple experiment tested implicit disability bias in language models. The model made biased assumptions when sentences were evaluated in one chat. It often associated nouns that were not accompanied by disability-related adjectives with positive situations, while the opposite was associated with negative scenarios. Finally, this work concludes by emphasizing the importance of adopting a careful and collaborative approach.

SEP 2021 • CONFERENCE PAPER

Heritage Education autochthonous' experience

TL;DR: Recife, the capital of Pernambuco, embodies a rich history through both its material traces and the collective imagination of its residents. This research explores how locals perceive urban spaces and cultural heritage, focusing on their experiences in the Santo Antônio and São José neighborhoods. Conducted through a remote digital ethnographic study, it examines how digital media can enhance Heritage Education and reshape the experience of walking in the city. A geolocalized system was created, featuring 60 points and 10 routes with media (photos, videos, audios) tied to memories and figures from the Dutch occupation (1630-1654). The findings show that geolocation can foster situated learning and highlights the challenges of designing digital systems in urban contexts marked by violence. Additionally, it suggests that such installations can facilitate narrative exchange and learning outside traditional settings like schools and museums, bringing heritage education into public spaces.
